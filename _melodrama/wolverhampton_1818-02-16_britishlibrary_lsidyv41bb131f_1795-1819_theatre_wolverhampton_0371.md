    - attraction: If the Dramas of Shakspeare were to be characterised, each by the
        particular excellence which distinguishes it from the rest, we must allow
        to the Tragedy of Hamlet the praise of variety. The dialogue throughout is
        nervous, emphatic, and striking ; each scene displays the Hero in a different
        situatioun and whether we behold him as a Lover, the Prince, the Son, the
        Soldier, or the Man, we find equal room for our utmost admiration. To prove
        the assertion, we need only select, from the many finely-drawn portraits of
        dramatic excellence with which this piece abounds, the awe-inspiring INTERVIEW
        with the GHOST of his FATHER, has religious observance of every direction
        given him through the medium of bis departed SIRE'S WANDERING SPIRIT, his
        subsequent Interviews with bis Mother, in which he produces the Assemblance
        of his Father and Uncle; his Eulogium on the spirits of the former, is a most
        severe though just detestation of the latter; and the sudden appearance of
        the Father, who comes to chide him for his delay in bringing his murderer
        to consign punishment, inclines us to pronounce the CLOSET SCENE A most perfect
        dramatic effort. The mournful Distraction of Ophelia fills the heart with
        tenderness; nor is there a person introduced but who produces every effect
        intended. New characters appear from time to time in continued succession,
        exhibiting various forms of life, and particular modes of conversation ; indeed,
        the incidents are so numerous, that the argument of the Play would make a
        large volume. The scenes are interchangeably diversified with merriment and
        solemnity, with merriment that includes judicious and instructive observations;
        and solemnity not strained by poetical violence above the natural sentiments
        of man; and when recorded testimony a vows it as the favourite production
        of our Immortal Bard, we presume it cannot have a greater claim upon the Public
        for every support and attention.
